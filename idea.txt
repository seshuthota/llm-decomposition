Yes. Here’s the detailed picture.

The literature does have papers that compare **SVD / low-rank compression** with **quantization**, but most of the strongest recent work is not a pure “SVD vs quantization only” duel. The field has moved toward three categories:

1. **Pure low-rank / SVD compression**
2. **Pure quantization**
3. **Hybrid methods** that use low-rank structure to improve quantization, or quantization to make low-rank factors cheaper ([arXiv][1])

## The cleanest way to think about it

* **Quantization** compresses by reducing the number of bits per weight or activation. GPTQ targets 3–4 bit weights with negligible degradation in its reported setting, SmoothQuant enables W8A8 PTQ, and AWQ is a strong weight-only PTQ method for 4-bit deployment. ([arXiv][2])
* **SVD / low-rank compression** compresses by replacing a large matrix (W) with smaller factors, e.g. (W \approx U_k \Sigma_k V_k^T) or (W \approx AB). SVD-LLM is the main 2024 reference point, and SVD-LLM V2 / AdaSVD are later refinements. ([arXiv][1])
* **Hybrid methods** explicitly combine both ideas. CALDERA models a layer as (W \approx Q + LR), where both the quantized component and low-rank factors matter; Low-Rank Correction for Quantized LLMs adds full-precision low-rank corrections on top of quantized models. ([arXiv][3])

## The papers that matter most

### 1. SVD-LLM (2024)

This is the foundational modern “pure SVD for LLM compression” paper. It argues that naïve SVD truncation is not enough, then adds truncation-aware whitening and a parameter-update step after truncation. It evaluates on 10 datasets and seven models, and positions itself as superior to prior SVD-based methods, especially at higher compression ratios. ([arXiv][1])

Why it matters for your question:
It is the main **pure SVD baseline** that later hybrid and improved SVD papers compare against. It is not a quantization paper, but it is the anchor for the low-rank side of the comparison. ([arXiv][1])

### 2. Characterizing the Accuracy–Efficiency Trade-off of Low-rank Decomposition in Language Models (2024)

This is a useful reality-check paper. It studies low-rank decomposition as a compression family in the broader landscape where quantization and pruning are also common options. In its reported experiments, it gets about a **9% model size reduction** with **4 to 10 percentage-point accuracy drops**, without retraining. ([arXiv][4])

Why it matters:
It shows that plain low-rank decomposition can help, but the gains may be modest unless the method is carefully optimized. That is one reason quantization stayed dominant for practical deployment. ([arXiv][4])

### 3. Quantization baselines: GPTQ, SmoothQuant, AWQ

These are not SVD papers, but they are the practical standards the low-rank world is implicitly competing with.

* **GPTQ**: one-shot weight quantization using approximate second-order information; reports 3–4 bit weights with negligible degradation and end-to-end inference speedups over FP16 in its experiments. ([arXiv][2])
* **SmoothQuant**: PTQ method that smooths activation outliers to enable W8A8 quantization; reports up to **1.56× speedup** and **2× memory reduction** with negligible accuracy loss. ([arXiv][5])
* **AWQ**: activation-aware weight-only quantization; protects salient channels and reports strong 4-bit deployment results plus more than **3× speedup** in its TinyChat system versus Hugging Face FP16 in the reported setup. ([arXiv][6])

Why they matter:
These methods explain why pure SVD has a high bar to clear. Quantization already offers aggressive compression, mature kernels, and real deployment tooling. ([arXiv][2])

### 4. CALDERA (2024)

This is one of the clearest hybrid papers. It approximates a weight matrix as

[
W \approx Q + LR
]

where (Q), (L), and (R) are all low-precision-constrained, and (LR) captures low-rank structure. CALDERA reports outperforming existing post-training LLM compression techniques in the regime of **less than 2.5 bits per parameter** on Llama-2 7B/13B/70B and Llama-3 8B. ([arXiv][3])

Why it matters:
This is one of the strongest signals that the field is moving beyond “quantization or SVD” toward “quantization plus low-rank structure.” ([arXiv][3])

### 5. Low-Rank Correction for Quantized LLMs (2024)

This paper is very directly on point. It targets **quantization errors**, especially for aggressive W4A4 settings, by adding low-rank full-precision correction matrices. It reports that with rank equivalent to **10%** of the original matrix size, the accuracy gap to the original model is reduced by **more than 50%**, and with rank at **30%**, the gap is reported as **closed completely** on tested models including Llama-2, Llama-3, Phi-3, and Mixtral. ([arXiv][7])

Why it matters:
This is probably the most explicit evidence that low-rank factors can be used as a “repair mechanism” for quantization damage rather than as a standalone competitor. ([arXiv][7])

### 6. SVD-LLM V2 (2025) and AdaSVD (2025)

These papers improve the SVD side rather than replacing it.

* **SVD-LLM V2** adds layer-specific compression-ratio allocation and loss-optimized truncation, reporting improvements over prior SVD-based methods. ([arXiv][8])
* **AdaSVD** adds adaptive compensation and adaptive layer-wise compression ratio selection, reporting stronger performance than prior SVD methods across tested model families. ([arXiv][9])

Why they matter:
They show that the low-rank side is improving, but they are still mostly competing within the SVD family rather than overturning quantization as the default deployment choice. ([arXiv][8])

## What the papers collectively suggest

### Main conclusion

There is no strong evidence from this set of papers that **pure SVD compression** has clearly displaced **pure quantization** as the default method for LLM deployment. Instead, the evidence points to this pattern:

* Quantization remains the most practical first-line compression tool because it delivers very strong memory savings and speedups with mature inference support. ([arXiv][2])
* Pure low-rank/SVD compression is useful, but getting strong results usually requires careful layer-wise rank allocation, truncation-loss modeling, and sometimes post-compression updates. ([arXiv][1])
* The most promising recent direction is hybrid: use quantization for the bulk of compression, and low-rank structure to model residuals, outliers, or quantization error. ([arXiv][3])

## Side-by-side summary

### Pure SVD / low-rank methods

**Best for:** exploiting redundancy in weight matrices, flexible per-layer compression, sometimes hardware-agnostic linear algebra.
**Weakness:** can lose quality quickly if rank is too low; choosing rank well is hard; benefits are often smaller than aggressive low-bit quantization unless the method is highly tuned. ([arXiv][1])

### Pure quantization

**Best for:** simple memory reduction, mature PTQ workflows, real inference speedups, strong hardware ecosystem.
**Weakness:** aggressive low-bit settings can create outlier and reconstruction errors, especially for activations. ([arXiv][2])

### Hybrid low-rank + quantization

**Best for:** extreme compression regimes where quantization alone becomes too destructive; correcting residual errors; pushing below comfortable quantization-only ranges.
**Weakness:** more complexity in optimization and deployment; may need extra calibration or mixed-precision handling. ([arXiv][3])

## The answer to your original question, very directly

Yes, there are research papers comparing them, but the comparison is usually **indirect or hybridized** rather than a single clean benchmark paper saying “SVD wins” or “quantization wins” universally. The most honest read is:

* **Quantization wins on practicality today**
* **Pure SVD is competitive in some regimes and is improving**
* **Hybrid methods are where a lot of the best recent results are landing** ([arXiv][2])
